Department-Wide Planning System (AHA! + GitHub)

I designed and rolled out a GitHub + AHA! planning system for a 9-team engineering department at Form3, replacing ad-hoc Lucid-based planning with capacity-based estimation, dependency tracking, and leadership reporting. When a later operations hire reviewed the work, it was adopted as the standard.

9 engineering teams planning on one system, matched against capacity in hours
2 hrs vs ~1 week to answer legal's tax-reporting data request, vs other departments
12 months in use as the department's standard, later adopted for additional teams

The problem

A 9-team department planned in Lucid. Estimates were rough and not tied to capacity, and there was no department-level view — so managers could not predict delivery or see dependencies, and there was no consistent way to roll up planning data for reporting.

I owned the design and rollout of a new planning and reporting system using GitHub and AHA!, working alongside teams already using both tools.

What I did

  1. Designed the integrated GitHub + AHA! workflow: how work was structured, how estimates were captured (in hours), how capacity was tracked per team, and how data rolled up to department level.
  2. Rolled it out across 9 teams, configuring for each team's planning rhythm.
  3. Built reports for two audiences: team-level (capacity vs planned work, surfacing over-commitment early) and department-level (a cross-team view of planned work, dependencies, and milestones, used mainly by the Head of Product and project managers).
  4. Acted as internal SME for AHA! configuration and governance.

Overcommitment became visible upfront — instead of after something had already slipped.

What changed

  • Concrete proof of the reporting layer: when the legal department requested project-level innovation allocation for tax reporting, my department completed it in ~2 hours — other departments took around a week.
  • Department-level planning visibility that did not exist before: all 9 teams' planned work in one view, matched against capacity in hours.
  • Overcommitment became visible and actionable — caught upfront rather than after slipping, and management had evidence-based capacity data.
  • The system was in use for ~12 months as the department's standard; when an operations team was later hired, they reviewed the workflow and adopted it as the standard for additional engineering teams.
