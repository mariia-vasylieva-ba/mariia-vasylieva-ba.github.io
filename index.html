Form3 · Payments infrastructure

Disaster Recovery Framework

The short version

I authored the DR guide that defined Form3's failure scenarios and testing approach. Form3 has since publicly reported zero customer impact during two major cloud outages (GCP 2025, AWS October 2025).

S

Situation

Form3's multi-cloud payments platform was still in active development. The system was designed for resilience, but there was no shared, documented view of which failure scenarios mattered, how the system should respond, and what clients should expect. Without that, the team could not test resilience systematically or communicate it to customers.

T

Task

I was responsible for authoring the Disaster Recovery User Guide: defining the failure scenarios the platform needed to handle, the system's response to each, and the client-facing plan of action.

A

Action

  • Mapped the full landscape of failure scenarios in a single diagram — infrastructure, network, regional, provider-level.
  • Ran scenario-by-scenario working sessions with architects and engineers to pressure-test each. Some were already handled; others required new work the engineering team picked up over time.
  • Negotiated which scenarios made the published guide, balancing comprehensiveness with what could honestly be committed to clients.
  • Authored the final guide as the single source of truth for DR scenarios and expected behaviour.
R

Result

  • The guide became the basis for Form3's regular DR testing — engineers simulate outages and validate recovery against the scenarios defined.
  • Form3 has publicly reported zero customer impact during two major cloud outages: a GCP outage earlier in 2025 and the October 2025 AWS US-East-1 outage.
  • The framework continues to underpin Form3's resilience testing approach.

Reducing Engineering Support Load

The short version

I analysed how 9 engineering teams at Form3 were spending their time on reactive support work. For one team, I redesigned the cross-team request workflow end-to-end, moving from Slack chaos to a GitHub-based system with templates, SLAs, and automation. Support work for that team dropped from approximately 80% to 20% of engineering time. The analysis pipeline I built scaled from 2 months (first team) to 2 hours per team for the remaining 8.

S

Situation

Form3 was growing rapidly; engineering teams flagged communication and workflow problems with other teams at retrospectives and team-building events. A previous BA attempt — shadowing team leaders for two weeks — produced some signal but not enough to act on. The problem was known but unquantified.

T

Task

I took on quantifying how engineering teams spent their time, identifying the biggest sources of reactive work, and designing changes to free up engineering capacity.

A

Action

  • Requested historical support-channel data across 9 engineering teams — 4,000+ Slack queries as the raw dataset.
  • Built a Python + Pandas analysis pipeline to categorise and quantify the support load per team. An engineer's initial script was the starting point; I rewrote and extended it substantially to answer the questions I needed.
  • Identified the biggest team-to-team sources of reactive work.
  • For one team, redesigned the cross-team request workflow end-to-end: replaced ad-hoc Slack requests with a GitHub-based system using templates, SLAs, and automation.
  • Scaled the analysis pipeline itself: the first team took ~2 months of manual work; by the end each of the remaining 8 teams took ~2 hours to analyse.
R

Result

  • For the redesigned team, support work dropped from approximately 80% to 20% of engineering time, measured by engineer self-reported time-per-request before and after.
  • The analysis approach became reusable across the department: 2 months → 2 hours per team.

Form3 · Product tooling

Department-Wide Planning System (AHA! + GitHub)

The short version

I designed and rolled out a GitHub + AHA! planning system for a 9-team engineering department at Form3, replacing ad-hoc Lucid-based planning with capacity-based estimation, dependency tracking, and leadership reporting. When a later operations hire reviewed the work, it was adopted as the standard.

S

Situation

A 9-team department planned in Lucid. Estimates were rough and not tied to capacity, and there was no department-level view, so managers could not predict delivery or see dependencies. There was no consistent way to roll up planning data for reporting.

T

Task

I owned the design and rollout of a new planning and reporting system using GitHub and AHA!, working alongside teams already using both tools.

A

Action

  • Designed the integrated GitHub + AHA! workflow: how work was structured, how estimates were captured (in hours), how capacity was tracked per team, how data rolled up to department level.
  • Rolled it out across 9 teams, configuring for each team's planning rhythm.
  • Built reports for two audiences: team-level (capacity vs planned work, surfacing over-commitment early) and department-level (a cross-team view of planned work, dependencies, and milestones, used mainly by the Head of Product and project managers).
  • Acted as internal SME for AHA! configuration and governance.
R

Result

  • Concrete proof of the reporting layer: when the legal department requested project-level innovation allocation for tax reporting, my department completed it in ~2 hours; other departments took around a week.
  • Department-level planning visibility that did not exist before: all 9 teams' planned work in one view, matched against capacity in hours.
  • Overcommitment became visible and actionable — caught upfront rather than after slipping; management had evidence-based capacity data.
  • The system was in use for ~12 months as the department's standard; when an operations team was later hired, they reviewed and adopted the workflow as the standard for additional engineering teams.

Luxoft · Regulated product, international stakeholders

KYC Configuration Across 20+ Countries

The short version

I worked on a KYC questionnaire platform for a global tier-1 bank, configuring the underlying BPMN engine to handle country-specific KYC processes across 20+ LATAM and APAC jurisdictions. Where the platform couldn't handle a regional requirement, I wrote the technical specifications for engineering to extend it.

S

Situation

A global tier-1 bank operated KYC processes that differed by jurisdiction. Each country's regime imposed its own questionnaire flow, fields, validation, and handoffs. The bank ran these on a BPMN-driven questionnaire platform that had to be configured per country.

T

Task

I configured the BPMN engine to handle country-specific KYC processes across 20+ LATAM and APAC jurisdictions, and captured requirements from regional stakeholders to get each country's process right.

A

Action

  • Worked with LATAM and APAC regional stakeholders to elicit each jurisdiction's KYC requirements.
  • Configured the BPMN questionnaire flows per country on the platform.
  • Where the platform could not handle a regional requirement out of the box, wrote the technical specifications for engineering to extend it.
R

Result

  • 20+ countries configured on a single BPMN platform for the bank.
  • Client-measured improvement in KYC cycle time.

About

Hello, I'm Mariia.

I'm a Senior Business Analyst who works at the point where engineering, operations, and product meet — the messy middle where things either get coordinated or quietly fall apart. Over 10+ years in fintech, payments, and enterprise tech, that's where I've found I'm most useful: turning unclear, fast-moving situations into systems and processes that hold up after I've moved on.

Most of my favourite work isn't glamorous. It's the disaster-recovery guide that becomes the basis for how a platform gets tested. The support-load analysis that frees an engineering team from drowning in Slack requests. The planning system that finally lets leadership see what's actually achievable. I care less about producing documents and more about whether something genuinely changed because I was there.

I also rebuilt my career from the ground up after moving to the UK, which, it turns out, is decent training for the job. Starting over in a new country teaches you to read a room quickly, ask the questions that feel obvious but aren't, and stay calm when nothing is yet familiar.

Outside work I'm usually elbow-deep in something hands-on. I bake sourdough, I'm slowly renovating my home myself, and I read a lot — 64 books last year, mostly modern prose, sci-fi and fantasy. There are also two cats who supervise all of the above, with mixed reviews.

What it's like working with me

I'm the person who stays calm when things get noisy. When a project is mid-chaos — unclear ownership, competing priorities, everyone talking past each other — that's usually when I'm most useful, not least.

A few things people tend to say about working with me:

  • I ask the awkward question early. Usually some version of "what problem are we actually solving here?" before we've spent three sprints building the wrong thing.
  • I think in systems, not just tasks. I can't help spotting the messy process, the missing source of truth, the thing that'll break at scale — and then quietly fixing it so it stays fixed.
  • I work credibly with engineers. I can read the API spec, follow the architecture conversation, and write the technical specification myself, so I'm a bridge, not a translation bottleneck.
  • I'm easy to work with. Analytical doesn't have to mean intense. I'm friendly, I keep things calm, and I'd rather solve a problem alongside you than hand it over a wall.
